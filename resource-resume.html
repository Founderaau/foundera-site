The Resume
Nobody Taught You

Direct and personal. Sounds like it came from someone who has been there. Because it did.

Most resume advice was written by people who have not looked at a resume pile recently. It is generic, outdated and weirdly focused on formatting when the real problem is almost always the content. This is the version that comes from the other side of the table. From the conversations nobody has on paper. Read it once. Use it every time.
0 of 10 sections read

The honest version. Without the softening.

Most resumes are ignored because they are indistinguishable from every other resume. Not because they are bad. Because they are all the same.

Every graduate writes bullet points the same way. Every resume has the same generic objective statement at the top. Every skills section lists Microsoft Office and "strong communication skills." None of it says anything. None of it is memorable. None of it gives a recruiter a reason to pause.

The resume that gets read is the one that makes a recruiter stop. The one that gives them something specific enough to remember. The one that sounds like a person wrote it, not a template generated it.

You have roughly six seconds before a recruiter decides whether to keep reading. Six seconds. They are not reading your bullet points. They are scanning for signals. The question you need to answer is: what signal is my resume sending in those six seconds?

If the answer is "nothing distinctive," you have a problem. The rest of this guide fixes that.

When a recruiter opens your resume, here is what they actually process first. In order.

Your name and contact details. Top of page. Easy to find. Professional email address. If your email is still from high school, change it before you send another application.
Your most recent role or university. And whether it is relevant to what they are hiring for.
How long you were there and what you did. Bullet points, not paragraphs. They are scanning, not reading.
One thing that stands out. A number. An achievement. A company name they recognise. Something specific.
The overall visual impression. Is this readable or am I working too hard to understand the layout?

That is it. If you do not have anything that stands out in step four, you are relying on the recruiter to care enough to dig deeper. Some will. Most will not.

What creates a stand-out in the scan

  • A quantified achievement ("Grew social engagement by 34% over three months")
  • A recognisable company or institution name
  • A relevant keyword that matches the job description
  • A clean layout that is easy to read at a glance

What kills the scan

  • Dense paragraphs instead of bullet points
  • A personal photo (do not include one in Australia unless specifically requested)
  • A two-page resume for under three years of experience
  • A "personal objective" paragraph that says nothing specific

This is the most important section in the guide. Read it twice.

Most people write bullet points that describe their job. Recruiters do not care what your job was. They care what you did and what it produced. There is a difference.

The formula: [Strong action verb] + [What you did] + [Result or scale]

Too weak

Assisted with social media management

Getting warmer

Managed Instagram and LinkedIn content for a retail brand with 12,000 followers

This lands

Created and scheduled weekly content across Instagram and LinkedIn, growing the overall engagement rate from 2.1% to 3.8% over four months

The difference is specificity and result. If you do not know the exact numbers, estimate and say so. "Supported a team of approximately 40 people" is better than "supported a large team." "Reduced processing time by roughly 20 percent" is better than "improved efficiency."

Common bullet point mistakes

Starting every bullet with "Responsible for." This is passive and weak. You were not just responsible for things. You did things. Lead with the action: Managed, Created, Analysed, Developed, Coordinated, Reduced, Grew, Built, Delivered.

Listing duties instead of contributions. The bullet "Attended weekly team meetings" tells a recruiter nothing. Everyone attends meetings. What did you contribute? What happened because of you?

Being vague when you could be specific. "Worked with stakeholders" could mean anything. "Coordinated across six internal teams to deliver a product launch on a tight timeline" means something.

Test: if your bullet points could appear on anyone's resume for a similar role, they are not doing their job. They need to be specific enough that they could only really be yours.

The skills section is usually the most wasted space on a resume. Two big mistakes are responsible for most of the damage.

Mistake 1: Listing soft skills as skills

"Strong communication skills." "Team player." "Problem solver." These are not skills. They are claims without evidence. Every single person applying for the same role will write the exact same things. Listing them does not differentiate you. It blends you in.

Soft skills belong in your bullet points, demonstrated through your experience. Not in a list that every recruiter has already learned to ignore.

Mistake 2: Listing things you barely know how to use

If a recruiter asked you to demonstrate your proficiency in something you have listed, could you? Confidently? If not, take it off. Getting caught overstating a skill in an interview is one of the most damaging things that can happen early in your career.

What actually belongs in your skills section

  • Software and tools (Adobe Suite, Salesforce, Xero, Python, Excel, Figma)
  • Languages, if you are genuinely proficient and it is relevant
  • Technical skills specific to your field
  • Certifications or licences that matter for the role

Every resume you send should be different. Not completely rewritten, but meaningfully different. The same resume sent to twenty jobs will get you fewer responses than twenty slightly different resumes. That is not an opinion. It is what the data from recruiters consistently shows.

The reason is simple: a hiring manager for an accounting role at Deloitte is not looking for the same signals as a hiring manager for a brand marketing role at a consumer goods company. They are solving different problems. Your resume needs to speak to their problem, not a generic version of your experience.

Tailoring to the specific job

Read the job description like a brief, not a checklist. What are they actually asking for? What language do they use? If they say "stakeholder management" and you have "client coordination," change your wording. ATS systems and human reviewers both scan for the terms from their own posting.

Lead with what is most relevant. Within each role, reorder your bullet points so the most applicable experience appears first. If the job emphasises financial analysis and you have a bullet about that buried third, move it to the top.

Adjust your skills section for every application. Remove skills that have no bearing on this role. If you are applying for a data role and you have "Adobe InDesign" listed, take it off. Every line should earn its place.

Tailoring to the specific industry

This is where most people miss the opportunity entirely. The structure of your experience matters less than how you frame it for the industry you are targeting.

If you are applying for an accounting role at a Big 4 firm or mid-tier practice, your resume should foreground anything that touches numbers, compliance, systems or client work. Even if you have never done formal accounting, find the thread. Did you manage a budget for a student society? Did you process invoices or reconcile expenses in a part-time job? Did you complete units in financial reporting or tax? That is your accounting story. Lead with it. Do not bury it under generic "strong analytical skills" claims. Name the software you have used: Xero, MYOB, Excel. Mention any relevant coursework: taxation, auditing, financial accounting. A recruiter for an accounting firm is looking for evidence of numerical rigour and attention to detail. Show that explicitly.

Generic, misses the target

Assisted finance team with administrative tasks and reporting. Strong attention to detail and organisational skills.

Accounting-specific, lands

Reconciled weekly expense reports and processed supplier invoices using Xero, identifying and correcting three duplicate payments totalling $2,400. Completed coursework in financial accounting, management accounting and taxation (average grade: Distinction).

If you are applying for a marketing or communications role, your resume should foreground creative output, campaign results, channel-specific skills and audience understanding. Quantify engagement, reach and conversion wherever possible. Name the platforms and tools you have used. Show that you understand brand voice and audience, not just task execution.

If you are applying for a consulting or strategy role, prioritise evidence of structured thinking, problem framing and quantified impact. Consulting firms are specifically looking for how you approach problems. Case competitions, research projects and analytical internships all belong front and centre.

If you are applying for a finance or banking role, technical skills are screened hard. Name your modelling experience, the software you use, the transactions or deals you have had exposure to. If you have completed CFA Level 1 or any Bloomberg certifications, list them. Academic performance matters more here than in most fields, so your WAM or GPA belongs on the page.

What to actually change versus what to leave alone

Change: The first bullet on each role, the order of bullet points within roles, the skills section, any language that does not match the job description.
Leave alone: Your layout, formatting, education section structure, and the core substance of your experience. Those are your base.

The test: read your tailored resume, then read the job description. Does your resume feel like it was written for this role? If someone who knows nothing about you read both, would they say "this person seems right for this job"? That is what you are aiming for. The tailoring process should take 15 to 30 minutes per application if your base resume is solid.

Almost every entry-level job posting lists experience requirements that entry-level candidates cannot possibly have. "One to two years experience required" on a graduate role is not a hard filter. It is a wish list. The person who wrote it probably knows they will hire a graduate. Apply anyway.

A job posting is a description of an ideal candidate that does not exist. If you meet 60 to 70% of what they are asking for, you belong in the running. Do not self-select out before the company has had the chance to do it for you.

What counts as experience

More than you think. The mistake most students make is assuming experience means paid, full-time, directly relevant work. It does not. Recruiters at graduate level are looking for evidence of capability, reliability and growth. That evidence can come from many places.

University clubs and societies. If you have held a position, managed a budget, run events, led a team or coordinated a project, that is real experience. Treat it like a job on your resume.
Part-time and casual work. Retail, hospitality, admin. Every role has transferable skills. Customer service builds communication and problem-solving under pressure. Shift supervision builds leadership. Cash reconciliation builds numerical accuracy. Find the thread.
Volunteering. If you have given sustained, committed time to an organisation, it counts. List it with the same structure as a job: what your role was, what you did, what the result was.
Internships, even short ones. Two weeks of work experience still belongs on the page if you did something real during it. Name the company, name what you did.
Academic projects and coursework. A semester-long research project, a group consulting assignment, a capstone, if it involved skills the role is asking for, it is relevant. Present it as a project entry, not just a course name.
Case competitions. If you have competed, list it. Even first-round participation demonstrates initiative and the ability to apply structured thinking under pressure.

How to frame transferable skills confidently

The challenge is not that you lack skills. It is that you have not yet done the work of connecting what you have done to what the employer needs. That connection is your job to make, not theirs.

Do not write "I have not worked in finance before, but..." That framing puts the gap front and centre. Instead, lead with what you have. Name the skill, show the evidence, let the relevance speak for itself.

Undersells it

No formal finance experience, but I am a fast learner and eager to develop skills in the industry.

Owns the transferable skill

Managed a $14,000 annual budget as Treasurer of the UNSW Finance Society, producing monthly P&L reports and presenting year-end financial summaries to a committee of 12.

The cover letter is where you bridge the gap

If your resume does not tick every box, the cover letter is your chance to address it directly, not apologetically, but confidently. Acknowledge what you are building toward and make the case for why what you bring right now is worth the conversation.

Something like: "I am completing my final year of a Bachelor of Commerce and have not yet held a formal accounting role. What I bring is strong academic grounding in financial reporting and taxation, practical experience managing financial records for a 200-member student organisation, and a clear intention to build a career in public practice." That is a better opening than most cover letters a recruiter will read that day.

Be honest. Be direct. Frame everything as relevant, because if you are applying, you believe it is. Do not apologise for being early in your career. Everyone in that hiring manager's team was early in their career once.

The students whose resumes stand out at graduation did not build them in final year. They built them across three or four years of small, deliberate decisions. The good news is that if you are reading this in first or second year, you still have time. If you are in third year, start immediately.

Join clubs and societies, then take a leadership role

Joining is fine. Being on the committee is better. Running something is best. Universities have commerce societies, law societies, investment clubs, consulting groups, marketing associations and dozens more. Pick one or two that are genuinely aligned with your career direction and commit to them properly. Get onto the committee. Take on a portfolio, events, sponsorships, education, marketing. The difference between "member" and "Vice President, Sponsorships" on a resume is significant, and the work to get there is entirely achievable.

Enter case competitions

Case competitions are one of the highest-return activities available to university students from a career-building perspective. They are free to enter, they build exactly the skills graduate employers screen for, structured problem solving, teamwork, communication under pressure, and they look strong on a resume regardless of whether you win. Many firms run their own competitions specifically as a talent pipeline. Deloitte, BCG, MinterEllison and others use competition results as a reason to fast-track applications. Enter early, enter often, and treat every one as practice.

Set up your LinkedIn profile from day one

Not in third year when you start applying. From the first week. Add your university, your major, your part-time roles as you take them, your society positions as you earn them. Connect with lecturers, classmates, guest speakers. A LinkedIn profile that has been building for three years looks different to one that was created two weeks before you sent your first application. Start it now and keep it current.

Seek out internships, even short ones, even unpaid

An unpaid two-week internship at a small firm is still a line on your resume. It is still a name, a context, a proof of initiative. Many students wait for formal internship programmes at large firms and miss out because those programmes are competitive and seasonal. In the meantime: reach out to small firms directly, ask your university's careers centre for contacts, ask family and family friends. A week at a suburban accounting firm still tells a recruiter that you sought out experience proactively rather than waiting for it to come to you.

Network while you are still studying

Informational interviews, where you ask someone already working in your target field for 20 minutes of their time to learn about their career, are one of the most underused tools available to students. Most professionals are willing to take them, particularly if you approach respectfully and come prepared with genuine questions. Attend industry events. Go to the panels your university hosts. Connect with alumni. The network you build as a student does not disappear when you graduate. It becomes the network you call on when you need a referral or a heads-up about a role.

Treat your academic results as a competitive asset

Your WAM or GPA is screened at entry for many graduate programmes, particularly in finance, law and consulting. Know what your WAM is. Know what the typical cutoff is for the programmes you want. If your results are strong, put them on your resume. If you made the Dean's List or received any academic award, list it. If you performed particularly well in units directly relevant to your target industry, you can note those individually. Academic results are one of the few things you can still improve while you are studying, and one of the few hard signals available when you have limited work experience.

The students who graduate with strong resumes are not necessarily smarter or luckier than their peers. They made a series of ordinary decisions, join the club, enter the competition, set up the LinkedIn, send the email, early enough that those decisions had time to compound. Start now.

This is specifically about what works in the Australian job market. Some of this is different from what you might read on US career advice sites.

Length: One page if you have under four years of experience. Two pages is fine if you genuinely have content to fill it. Three pages is rarely justified for anyone under 35.
Font: Something clean and modern. Inter, Calibri, Gill Sans. Not Times New Roman. Not fancy script. Not anything that makes you look like you are trying too hard.
Margins: Standard, around 2cm. Do not shrink them to fit more in. If it does not fit, cut the content.
Contact details: Email, LinkedIn, phone number. Location is optional but helps if applying to a specific city. Do not include your full home address.
No photo. Unlike some European countries, photos are not standard in Australia and can cause issues. Leave it off unless specifically asked for.
Colour: Minimal. One accent colour at most, used sparingly. Black and white reads as more professional than you think.
No tables or text boxes. They cause problems with ATS software that many companies use to filter resumes before a human sees them. A simple layout without them works best.

The final check most people skip. Do not skip it.

Read it out loud

Everything that sounds awkward when you say it out loud needs to be rewritten. Your resume should sound like a competent, thoughtful person wrote it, not a document generator.

Run the plain text test

Copy and paste your resume into Notepad or TextEdit, which strips all formatting. If it becomes completely unreadable, an ATS will struggle with it too. Clean it up.

Verify every claim

Could you talk confidently about every single thing on this resume if asked in an interview? If there is anything you would feel uncomfortable defending, take it off or reframe it honestly.

Get a second pair of eyes

Ask someone who does not know your work history to read it. Then ask them: "After reading this, what do I do and what am I good at?" If they cannot answer clearly, your resume is not clear enough.

Send it as a PDF

Unless specifically told otherwise. A PDF looks identical on every device. A Word document does not.

Apply even when you do not meet 100% of the criteria. If you meet 60 to 70% of what they are asking for, apply. Hiring managers write a wish list, not a mandatory specification. The resume gets you the conversation. The conversation gets you the role.
